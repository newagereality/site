Similarities Betwen What Was Said About Musa and Gilgamesh.

These indicate that the ancient Middle Easterners received prophets who taught them about Musa, or that they received the message of Islam and distorted what was taught to them.

Their Stories

The following linked article finds many similarities, but makes the mistake of believing that this means Musa is copied from Gilgamesh. Rather, it is the other way around.

-- MOSES/GILGAMESH --

Gilgamesh: Journeys to the "mouth of the rivers" (the edge of the world) to find Utnapishtim, the survivor of the Great Flood, to seek the secret of immortality.

Musa: Vows to travel until he reaches the "Junction of the Two Seas" (Majma’ al-Bahrayn). He is willing to spend "ages" (huquba) to reach al-Khidr (who is said to live up to the end of times).

The Sleep Test: Utnapishtim tells Gilgamesh that if he wants immortality, he must stay awake for six days and seven nights. Gilgamesh fails immediately.

The Patience Test: al-Khidr tells Moses, "You will not be able to have patience with me." He then performs three seemingly "wrong" acts. Musa fails the test by questioning al-Khidr.

Gilgamesh would sleep with every bride on their wedding night before their husbands.

The Jews said about Musa:

• Rabbi Samuel son of Nachmeyni says that he had heard that these people suspected him of committing adultery (Baal HaTurim) since we read in the verse "And they envied Moses in the camp" (Psalms 106:16) which is interpreted by Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzchak as meaning that each Jewish husband suspected Moses of adultery with his wife.

This hadith indicates that there was confusion about the identities of legendary figures that were perverted stories of Musa.

Narrated Sa`id bin Jubair: I said to Ibn `Abbas:

"Nauf Al-Bukah claims that Musa, the companion of Al-Khadir was not Musa (the prophet) of the children of Israel, but some other Musa." Ibn `Abbas said, "Allah's enemy (i.e. Nauf) has told a lie."

Sahih al-Bukhari 3401

It is possible that he was confused from the stories of GilgaMoshe.

Gilgamesh fights and slays lions and wolves during his journey to the Cedar Forest to protect shepherds and their flocks. He uses his axe and sword.

In a Judeo-Persian poetic retelling of his life, Musa fights a "black lion" and a wolf while guarding Shu'ayb's flocks. He strikes the lion with his staff, seizes it, and smashes its body to the ground.

Gilgamesh killed the 'Bull of Heaven' sent by the deity Ishtar. This could parallel the destruction of the golden calf idol.
